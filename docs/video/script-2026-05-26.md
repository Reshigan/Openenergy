---

## Editorial principle — read this before changing anything

This is a **product walkthrough and sales pitch**, not a technical tour. The
narrator is the company's lead product person, sitting next to a buyer,
talking through what the product does and what they get.

- Every beat is **one flowing paragraph**, never split with mid-thought
  blank lines. Internal blank `>` lines in blockquotes create half-second
  pauses in TTS that read as "staggered" delivery, so we avoid them inside
  a beat. Use sentence-final `.` for the natural breath.
- **Business language only.** No platform plumbing, no acronyms beyond the
  product name itself, no protocol names, no regulator-speak that needs a
  glossary. "Renewable energy certificate" is fine; "I-REC" is not. "The
  national energy regulator" is fine; "NERSA" is not (unless naming SA).
- **Every beat answers two questions:** what can a person *do* here, and
  what do they *get back* for doing it? Lead with the doing, close with
  the value.
- **Present tense, ready-state.** The system is in production at
  oe.vantax.co.za today. Never "we will", never "coming soon", never
  "pilot" or "proof-of-concept".

## ACT 2 — The product walkthrough (1:20 – 4:30)

### Beat 2.1 — One workspace, every workflow (1:20 – 1:55) `[SCREEN: cec-launch-board-tour]`
`[CHYRON: Launch boards // a personal home page for every role]`

> When you sign in to the Cockpit, the first thing you see is your launch board — a single home page tuned to your role. The trader sees the order book and today's profit and loss. The lender sees the portfolio they fund and the projects that need attention. The off-taker sees their power purchase agreements, their pipeline of new deals, and their carbon footprint. The grid operator sees the national frequency in real time. Every launch board pulls in the three or four numbers that matter most to that role, the workflows they touch every day, and the suggestions the system has made for them overnight.

### Beat 2.2 — Trading made simple (1:55 – 2:25) `[SCREEN: trading-order-book-energy, trading-trade-blotter]`
`[CHYRON: Trade // power, certificates, carbon, capacity]`

> Trading on the Cockpit feels like booking a flight. You see what is on offer, when it is available, and what it costs. Day-ahead. Intraday. Forward. Renewable energy certificates. Carbon credits. Capacity to keep the grid balanced. Every trade settles automatically. Every position updates in real time. And before you ever submit, the Cockpit checks the order against your credit, your exposure, your hedging mandate, and the market rules — and tells you in plain English if anything is off.

### Beat 2.3 — Build a project, end to end (2:25 – 3:00) `[SCREEN: ipp-projects-list, ipp-project-detail-financial-model]`
`[CHYRON: Build // from feasibility to twenty years of operations]`

> A new solar farm, a wind site, a hybrid plant with batteries, a small hydro scheme — each one lives on the Cockpit as a single project file. Feasibility, bankability, construction, commissioning, and then twenty years of operations. The financial model, the power purchase agreement, the licence papers, the construction milestones, the environmental approvals — all in one place, all versioned, all replayable. When a developer asks for a payment, every document the lender needs is already attached. Approval becomes a decision, not a paper chase.

### Beat 2.4 — Money in, money out (3:00 – 3:30) `[SCREEN: lender-drawdown-queue, settlement-cleared-trades]`
`[CHYRON: Settle // invoices, drawdowns, payments]`

> Power purchase agreements settle on schedule. Every cleared megawatt-hour becomes an invoice and a payment instruction. Lender drawdowns flow through a live health check. If a project is healthy, the money moves the same day. If it is not, the Cockpit shows the developer exactly what to fix in language a borrower and a banker can both understand. There is no spreadsheet hidden in someone's inbox. The numbers in the contract are the numbers on the dashboard.

### Beat 2.5 — Oversight built in (3:30 – 4:00) `[SCREEN: regulator-surveillance-alerts, grid-congestion-map]`
`[CHYRON: Oversight // a regulator's window into the market]`

> The national energy regulator has its own workstation on the same platform. It sees fair pricing, fair access, and any pattern that looks unusual. If something needs a closer look, the regulator opens a review with one click, and the full story is right there for them. On the grid side, the system operator sees frequency, voltage, and congestion across every part of the country in real time. When something goes wrong, every party affected sees the same data at the same moment. No phone calls. No conflicting reports. One shared picture.

### Beat 2.6 — Trust everyone can see (4:00 – 4:30) `[SCREEN: public-audit-page-roots]`
`[CHYRON: Trust // a verified history of every action]`

> Every action on the Cockpit — every trade, every payment, every approval, every change of state — is written into a verified history that anyone can inspect. Lenders. Auditors. Treasury. The minister's office. Civil society. The public. When you can show your work, you don't have to explain yourself twice. That single property is what makes a multi-stakeholder market actually work.

## ACT 3 — A day in each role (4:30 – 11:00)

### Beat 3.1 — The trader (4:30 – 5:15) `[SCREEN: trader-workstation, trader-place-order, trader-ai-suggestion-accept, trader-feature-tour]`
`[CHYRON: Trader // power, certificates, carbon — one desk]`

> Meet Sipho. Sipho trades power for an independent producer. He signs in once, and his launch board tells him what changed overnight — open orders, filled trades, his profit and loss, his carbon position. He drafts an order — fifty megawatt-hours of day-ahead baseload — and the Cockpit suggests a price band along with the reasoning. Recent clearing prices. Expected wind drop-off this evening. His current exposure. One click to accept. Behind his trading desk sits a full risk console, a renewable certificate book, a carbon credit ledger, a pre-trade compliance check, and a direct line to the regulator's market view. Everything Sipho needs to act, and everything his firm needs to prove it acted correctly — all in one place.

### Beat 3.2 — The project developer (5:15 – 6:00) `[SCREEN: ipp-workstation, ipp-project-detail-financial-model, ipp-drawdown-request, ipp-feature-tour]`
`[CHYRON: Project developer // build, commission, operate]`

> Naledi runs a one-hundred-megawatt solar plant in the Northern Cape. Her workstation gives her a live view of generation, planned outages, contractor jobs, the price her power is selling at, and the next scheduled payment from her lender. She requests forty-two million rand against the substation handover milestone. The Cockpit runs the health check, packages the supporting evidence, and forwards it on. What used to take two weeks of email and PDF chasing now takes a single afternoon. The same workspace also handles her financial model, her power purchase agreement library, her environmental compliance, her workforce safety register, and her carbon disclosures — so the team that builds the plant is the same team that operates it, on the same screen.

### Beat 3.3 — The lender (6:00 – 6:45) `[SCREEN: lender-workstation, lender-covenant-dashboard, lender-watchlist-alert, lender-feature-tour]`
`[CHYRON: Lender // every loan, every day, one screen]`

> Thandi runs the credit portfolio at her bank. Her cockpit shows every loan she finances, ranked by how close it is to needing attention. One borrower is on the watchlist this morning. Not in trouble — but worth a conversation. The Cockpit has already pulled what she needs: latest financials, a scenario showing what happens if revenue dips, a draft letter ready for her to send. She approves. The borrower hears from her the same morning. Behind that single decision sits the whole credit suite — covenants tracked continuously, a live disbursement queue, a watchlist driven by signals from the asset itself, a dunning workflow when things slip, and a board pack that builds itself for the next credit committee.

### Beat 3.4 — The off-taker (6:45 – 7:45) `[SCREEN: offtaker-workstation, offtaker-bill-upload, offtaker-ai-analytics, offtaker-procurement-rfp, offtaker-loi-list, offtaker-loi-accept, offtaker-contract-sign, offtaker-feature-tour]`
`[CHYRON: Off-taker // buy renewable power, on your terms]`

> Thabo runs energy procurement for a large industrial customer. He drops his last electricity bill into the Cockpit. The platform reads it — annual draw, time-of-use split, demand charges, tariff exposure — all lifted out of the PDF in seconds. Then the system does the harder work. It scans every operating, in-build, and financial-close project on the network and proposes a blend. Two solar farms here. A wind site there. The right battery alongside. One hundred and twenty gigawatt-hours a year, over fifteen years. Renewable. Bankable. Climate reporting baked in. Thabo sends letters of indent to the producers he likes, and when one is signed, the term sheet drafts itself the same moment. He signs the contract on the platform — name typed, consent confirmed — and the legal record is written. Behind that single act of buying power sits the full off-taker suite — a procurement workspace, a contracts library, a settlement queue, a billing helpdesk, and a carbon disclosure cockpit, all sharing the same data.

### Beat 3.5 — The carbon fund (7:45 – 8:20) `[SCREEN: carbon-portfolio, carbon-issuance-queue, carbon-feature-tour]`
`[CHYRON: Carbon fund // verified credits, listed and retired]`

> The carbon fund manages a portfolio of verified offset projects. Every cleared megawatt-hour from a renewable source automatically generates a renewable energy certificate. Issuance. Listing. Trade. Retirement. Each step is on the record, and the moment a certificate is retired it cannot be sold again. By design. Behind the portfolio sits a full registry — project files, verifier sign-offs, monitoring evidence, listing rules, a marketplace for trade, and a retirement workflow that closes the loop back to the buyer's annual carbon report.

### Beat 3.6 — The regulator (8:20 – 9:00) `[SCREEN: regulator-workstation, regulator-surveillance-rules, regulator-investigation-open, regulator-feature-tour]`
`[CHYRON: Regulator // license, surveil, decide]`

> The regulator's workstation surfaces two unusual market patterns overnight. Both have been triaged by the duty officer. One opens as a formal review. Every step — who looked, when, what they saw — is recorded, and the eventual decision file is signed, dated, and in the exact format the regulator needs to publish. The same workstation handles licence applications, tariff filings from producers and grid utilities, enforcement actions, and a public-comment desk for stakeholder submissions. The regulator runs the market without ever leaving the screen.

### Beat 3.7 — The grid operator (9:00 – 9:40) `[SCREEN: grid-operator-workstation, grid-frequency-live, grid-curtailment-event, grid-feature-tour]`
`[CHYRON: Grid operator // a live national picture]`

> The grid operator opens the morning to frequency within band, voltage nominal, and three congestion zones active in the inland corridor. A curtailment was instructed in zone two an hour ago. The Cockpit has already worked out what is owed — three hundred and forty thousand rand to two affected producers — and queued the payment for tonight. Reserve margin, dispatch instructions, ancillary services, outage planning, redispatch costs, the whole national system view — all sharing the same numbers the producers, the buyers, and the regulator see. There is no separate ledger to reconcile after the fact.

### Beat 3.8 — The asset operator (9:40 – 10:25) `[SCREEN: esums-site-list, esums-site-detail-live, esums-opportunity-feed, esums-work-order-detail, esums-feature-tour]`
`[CHYRON: Asset operator // every plant working at its best]`

> Underneath the trading and the contracts and the financing sits Esums. Esums is the operating brain for solar plants, wind sites, batteries, and water systems. It listens to what every piece of equipment is doing and quietly looks for ways to make it earn more. Soiling. Inverter clipping. Module ageing. Battery health. Water-pump waste. Every finding becomes a rand value and a job for the field team. Today, across the live operating fleet, four point one million rand of recoverable upside, already queued as work. The asset operator works in the same Cockpit as everyone else — so when Esums lifts a plant's output, the lender's health check, the buyer's carbon report, and the trader's day-ahead position all update without anyone re-typing a number.

### Beat 3.9 — Esums up close (10:25 – 11:00) `[SCREEN: esums-site-detail-live, esums-opportunity-feed, esums-work-order-detail]`
`[CHYRON: Esums // findings become work, work becomes value]`

> Take one finding from this morning. Site Bravo, in the Northern Cape, lost two point eight percent of its yield last month. The cause — a single inverter drifting out of calibration. The system spotted the pattern three weeks before the on-site crew would have. One tap, and a work order is created with the right part on the bill of materials. The technician arrives prepared. The lender's monthly health check already accounts for the recovery. The buyer's performance report writes itself. Each finding is a ticket, each ticket carries a value, and each value stays on the dashboard until the field team closes it out.
