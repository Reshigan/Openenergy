# Consolidated Energy Cockpit (CEC) — 15-minute product film

**Audience:** United Nations + Global ESCO Network
**Duration:** 15:00 (target)
**Voice:** en-US-AriaNeural (Microsoft Edge TTS — warm, professional female, en-US)
**Pace:** ~150 wpm deliberate
**Word budget:** ~2,250 spoken words
**Visual:** SPA captures at 1920×1080, 30 fps, headed Chromium via Playwright
**Composite:** ffmpeg — V/O over screen + lower-third role chyron, navy/emerald/gold lower-thirds

---

## Production conventions

- Timing markers `[mm:ss]` are absolute from t=0.
- Screen cues `[SCREEN: <spec-name>]` map 1:1 to a `tests/video/<role>.spec.ts` shot key.
- Lower-third chyrons `[CHYRON: title // subtitle]` appear over the corresponding shot.
- Narrator pauses are written as ellipses `…` (~250ms each); paragraph breaks are ~500ms.
- Numbers spelled out where they affect TTS phrasing (e.g. "twenty thirty" not "2030").
- All on-screen data is the anonymized seed from migration 079 (Phase 1).

## ACT 2 — Why this matters for the Global South (1:30 – 3:00)

### Beat 2.1 — The grid reality (1:30 – 2:05) `[SCREEN: grid-frequency-chart-live]`
`[CHYRON: Grid frequency // forty-nine point nine-seven hertz]`

> The South African grid runs at fifty hertz nominal.
> When generation falls behind demand — frequency drops. Below forty-nine point five and load-shedding begins.
> In twenty twenty-three, this country lost over three hundred days to rolling blackouts. The economic cost: more than four hundred billion rand.
> A modern energy exchange has to do more than match buyers and sellers. It has to keep the frequency stable. Predict congestion. Settle in minutes, not months.

### Beat 2.2 — The financing reality (2:05 – 2:40) `[SCREEN: lender-portfolio-watchlist]`
`[CHYRON: Capital deployed // one point two four billion rand AUM]`

> Project finance for renewables in Africa is a paperwork industry.
> A typical independent power producer can wait six to nine months between financial close and first drawdown.
> Lenders demand covenants — debt service coverage, availability, insurance — but the data to monitor those covenants … lives in a hundred different spreadsheets.
> The Consolidated Energy Cockpit puts every covenant on a live dial. With automatic breach detection, structured reason codes, and a regulator-grade audit chain behind every draw request.

### Beat 2.3 — The transparency reality (2:40 – 3:00) `[SCREEN: public-audit-page-roots]`
`[CHYRON: Hash-chained audit // Ed25519 signed daily]`

> Every action on this platform … is recorded in a tamper-evident audit chain.
> Daily Merkle roots are signed, published, and available to anyone on the public transparency portal.
> Civil society. Auditors. Treasury. The public.
> Transparency is not an export … it is the substrate.

**Act 2 runtime:** 1:30 · ~225 words

## ACT 3 — The platform in one minute per pillar (3:00 – 6:00)

### Beat 3.1 — Producers and projects (3:00 – 3:45) `[SCREEN: ipp-projects-list, ipp-project-detail-financial-model]`
`[CHYRON: Producers // four IPPs, four hundred and ten megawatts]`

> Pillar one — the producers.
> Solar in the Northern Cape. Wind on the Eastern Cape coast. Hybrid plants with battery storage. Run-of-river hydro.
> Each project lives as a single object on the platform. From feasibility — through bankability — to commissioning — to twenty-year operations.
> Financial models. Bankable PPAs. Lender drawdown schedules. NERSA licence applications. Construction milestones. Environmental impact assessments.
> Every artefact — versioned, hash-stamped, and replayable to any point in the project's life.
> A regulator can step into the project's history three years after commissioning and replay the exact state of the file the day the licence was granted.
> That is not nice to have. That is the prerequisite for cross-border capital flowing into Global South renewables at the scale this transition demands.

### Beat 3.2 — Markets (3:45 – 4:30) `[SCREEN: trading-order-book-energy, trading-trade-blotter]`
`[CHYRON: Markets // power, carbon, RECs, capacity]`

> Pillar two — the markets.
> A continuous limit order book for electricity. Day-ahead, intraday, and forward.
> A carbon market for verified offsets and renewable energy certificates.
> A capacity market for firming services.
> Every order is screened in real time. Credit limit. Exposure. Mark-to-market sanity. Trading halts. K-Y-C status.
> An order that fails any pre-trade guard is rejected with a structured reason code — and an inline A-I explanation of what went wrong, and how to fix it.

### Beat 3.3 — Capital and settlement (4:30 – 5:15) `[SCREEN: settlement-cleared-trades, lender-drawdown-queue]`
`[CHYRON: Capital // PPA settlement, drawdowns, dunning]`

> Pillar three — capital.
> Power purchase agreements settle on a daily cron at ten minutes past midnight — every cleared MWh, multiplied by the indexed tariff, generates an invoice and a settlement instruction.
> Lender drawdowns flow through a covenant engine. If debt-service coverage drops below one point two — the draw request enters a covenant-breach workflow, with structured reason codes, dunning calendar, and lender consent gates.
> Margin calls. Variation margin. Default management. All running on the same audit chain.

### Beat 3.4 — Oversight and grid (5:15 – 6:00) `[SCREEN: regulator-surveillance-alerts, grid-congestion-map]`
`[CHYRON: Oversight // NERSA-aligned surveillance + grid telemetry]`

> Pillar four — oversight.
> NERSA has direct, read-only access to a regulator workstation.
> Market surveillance rules run every fifteen minutes — wash-trade detection, marking-the-close, layering, spoofing.
> Alerts are triaged. Investigations open with a single click. Every regulator decision is published to the public transparency log within twenty-four hours of issuance.
> Civil society can subscribe. Academics can subscribe. The public can subscribe.
> And on pillar five — the grid operator sees real-time frequency, voltage, and congestion across every constrained zone … with wheeling charges, imbalance settlement, and emergency curtailment all wired into the same ledger.
> When a grid event happens — a sudden frequency excursion, a transmission fault — the cockpit captures it, the regulator sees it, and the affected producers see it … at the same moment, with the same data.

**Act 3 runtime:** 3:00 · ~470 words

## ACT 4 — A walk through each role (6:00 – 10:30)

### Beat 4.1 — Trader workstation (6:00 – 6:45) `[SCREEN: trader-workstation, trader-place-order, trader-ai-suggestion-accept]`
`[CHYRON: Trader desk // Sipho Nkomo, Vanta X Capital]`

> Meet Sipho.
> Sipho trades power for a mid-tier independent producer. He logs in once. Two-factor. Step-up M-F-A for any order over fifty megawatts.
> The launch board shows him what changed since he last left. Open orders. Filled trades. Mark-to-market profit and loss. Carbon position.
> He drafts an order — fifty megawatt-hours, day-ahead, base load. The cockpit's inline A-I suggests a price band one rand sixty above the live mark.
> The reasoning is right there — recent clearing prices, expected wind drop-off, his current exposure to that delivery date.
> One click to accept. The order routes to the order book. Sipho moves on.

### Beat 4.2 — IPP developer (6:45 – 7:30) `[SCREEN: ipp-workstation, ipp-project-financial-model, ipp-drawdown-request]`
`[CHYRON: IPP developer // Naledi Khumalo, Solar IPP zero one]`

> Naledi runs a one-hundred-megawatt solar plant in the Northern Cape.
> Her project workstation gives her a live view of everything that matters — generation today, planned outages, contractor work orders, the indexed PPA tariff, the next drawdown.
> She requests a drawdown of forty-two million rand against milestone five — substation handover.
> The covenant engine checks her debt-service coverage … one point three eight. Above the one-point-two minimum.
> Insurance certificate — current. Availability covenant — ninety-six point two percent over the trailing twelve months.
> The drawdown moves to lender consent — automatically — with the supporting pack already packaged.

### Beat 4.3 — Lender (7:30 – 8:15) `[SCREEN: lender-workstation, lender-covenant-dashboard, lender-watchlist-alert]`
`[CHYRON: Lender // Thandi Mokoena, Vanta X Credit]`

> Thandi runs the credit portfolio.
> Her cockpit gives her every covenant on every loan, ranked by proximity to breach.
> The covenant engine flagged a borrower this morning — debt-service coverage drifting toward one point two five. Not yet a breach … but on the watchlist.
> Thandi opens the borrower's file. Last quarter's audited financials are already attached. The cockpit has run a sensitivity — what happens to coverage if the indexed tariff drops three percent. Or curtailment rises five percent.
> She schedules a conversation. The cockpit drafts the letter. She approves. The borrower receives it … and a structured update appears in the borrower's launch board.

### Beat 4.4 — Carbon fund (8:15 – 8:45) `[SCREEN: carbon-portfolio, carbon-issuance-queue]`
`[CHYRON: Carbon fund // verified offsets + RECs]`

> Carbon-Fund.
> Renewable Energy Certificates are issued automatically on every cleared megawatt-hour from accredited renewable generation.
> Verified emission reductions flow through a four-stage workflow — issuance, listing, trade, retirement.
> Every retirement is published. Double-counting is impossible by construction.

### Beat 4.5 — Regulator (8:45 – 9:30) `[SCREEN: regulator-workstation, regulator-surveillance-rules, regulator-investigation-open]`
`[CHYRON: Regulator // NERSA oversight workstation]`

> The regulator workstation.
> Wash-trade detection. Marking-the-close. Layering. Spoofing.
> Two alerts opened overnight. Both have been triaged by the regulator's compliance officer. One has been escalated to a formal investigation.
> Every step is on the audit chain. The investigation file is signed, time-stamped, and exportable in NERSA's required format.
> Outside investigation hours, the same regulator workstation gives a live read of licensed entities, tariff applications under review, and the public submissions queue.

### Beat 4.6 — Grid operator (9:30 – 10:00) `[SCREEN: grid-operator-workstation, grid-frequency-live, grid-curtailment-event]`
`[CHYRON: Grid operator // live frequency + congestion]`

> The grid operator.
> Frequency — forty-nine point nine-seven hertz, well within band. Voltage — nominal. Three active congestion zones in the inland transmission corridor.
> A curtailment event triggered in zone two an hour ago. The cockpit has already routed the compensation calculation — three hundred and forty thousand rand owed to two affected IPPs. Settlement instruction queued for the next overnight cycle.

### Beat 4.7 — Off-taker (10:00 – 10:30) `[SCREEN: offtaker-workstation, offtaker-procurement-rfp]`
`[CHYRON: Off-taker // industrial procurement]`

> And the off-taker.
> A large industrial buyer. An open request-for-proposal for one hundred and twenty gigawatt-hours per year over fifteen years. Renewable. Bankable. With ESG reporting baked in.
> Bids come in through the marketplace. The cockpit scores them on price, on carbon intensity, on counterparty credit, and on contract compatibility. The buyer sees a single ranked list. The signature draft is generated automatically. The audit trail is on chain from the moment the RFP opens.

## ACT 5 — How institutions plug in (10:30 – 13:00)

### Beat 5.1 — Esums asset intelligence (10:30 – 11:15) `[SCREEN: esums-site-list, esums-site-detail-live, esums-opportunity-feed]`
`[CHYRON: Esums // operational intelligence layer]`

> Underneath all of this … is Esums.
> Esums is the asset intelligence layer. It ingests solar, wind, battery, and water telemetry directly from the inverters and S-C-A-D-A — no third-party gateway.
> Thirteen quantified opportunity detectors run on the live data.
> Soiling losses. Inverter clipping. Module degradation. Battery state-of-health drift. Curtailment leakage. Water-pump cycling inefficiency.
> Each detection is converted into a recoverable rand value. Today across the demo fleet — four point one million rand of recoverable upside identified and queued as work orders.

### Beat 5.2 — UN ESCO integration model (11:15 – 12:15) `[SCREEN: esums-portal-share-token, public-audit-page-proof]`
`[CHYRON: ESCO integration // tokenized portal access]`

> For the Global ESCO Network … here is how it works.
> An Energy Service Company onboards as a tenant. Their fleet — wherever it is in the world — connects to the cockpit through native telemetry adapters.
> Each ESCO project gets a tokenized portal. Their lender sees what their lender needs to see. Their off-taker sees what their off-taker needs to see. Their insurer sees the availability covenant. Their contractor sees the work order queue.
> Nothing more. Nothing less. Every access … logged. Every share-token expires.
> The audit chain travels with the data. So an institutional investor in Geneva can verify the production of a thirty-megawatt site in Lusaka — with cryptographic certainty — without ever speaking to the operator.

### Beat 5.3 — Sovereign and multilateral hooks (12:15 – 13:00) `[SCREEN: public-legal-page-applications, regulator-decisions-list]`
`[CHYRON: Sovereign hooks // regulator + treasury read-only]`

> For sovereign deployments … the regulator workstation can be cloned for a national energy regulator anywhere it is needed. Each instance carries its own legal mappings — licensing regime, tariff methodology, statutory reporting cadence.
> For multilateral institutions — UNDP, UNECE, the African Development Bank, the World Bank — read-only consoles publish aggregate market data, capital flows, and compliance scoring. No identifiable counterparty data. Just the verifiable signals needed for portfolio oversight.
> For climate-finance instruments — the Green Climate Fund, the Climate Investment Funds — every disbursement, every milestone, every avoided ton of carbon … is verifiable against the same audit chain that the borrower's lender uses.
> POPIA, GDPR, the SADC data protection model — all enforced at the row level. Tenant isolation runs on every query. Data sovereignty is configurable per region.
> When the rules change — and they will — the cockpit changes with them.
